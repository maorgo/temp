## [The Insurgency Threatening to Derail a U.S.-Pakistan Pact](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/05/03/world/asia/pakistan-balochistan-insurgency-reko-diq.html)
**Source:** NYTimes | **Published:** 2026-05-03 16:40

- The Trump administration announced $1.3 billion in investments in Pakistan's Balochistan Province, targeting large gold and copper reserves, particularly the Reko Diq copper-gold mine, following diplomatic outreach by Pakistan's Army chief Field Marshal Syed Asim Munir.
- The Baloch Liberation Army (B.L.A.), a separatist militant group, poses a direct threat to these U.S. mining ambitions, having carried out hundreds of increasingly sophisticated attacks in recent years.
- On January 31, 2026, approximately 500 B.L.A. militants launched a coordinated assault striking at least 18 targets across 12 areas in Balochistan, killing at least 58 people — the group's most expansive attack in years.
- The January 31 attacks targeted not only military and police installations but also civilian targets, including a suicide bombing near the Balochistan chief minister's residence and attacks on banks, representing an escalation in the insurgency's scope.
- Pakistan has sought to attract U.S. investment as a counterweight to Chinese dominance in the region's mining sector, while also positioning itself as a mediator between the U.S. and Iran.

> The escalating Baloch separatist insurgency poses a significant security risk to the Trump administration's $1.3 billion mining investment deal in Pakistan's Balochistan Province, potentially undermining a key element of U.S.-Pakistan strategic and economic cooperation.

## [Jordanians Struggle as Mideast Wars Scare Tourists Away](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/05/02/world/middleeast/jordan-travel-tourists-war.html)
**Source:** NYTimes | **Published:** 2026-05-02 09:00

- Jordan's tourism high season was nearly wiped out due to regional conflicts in the Middle East.
- Despite Jordan largely staying out of the fighting, the country suffered significant tourism losses.
- Visitors canceled flights, hotel bookings, and tours en masse.

**Additional context:**
- Tourism accounts for roughly 14–15% of Jordan's GDP and supports approximately 1 in 8 jobs in the country, making the sector's collapse due to regional conflict a major macroeconomic blow, not just an industry setback.
- Petra, Jordan's most-visited UNESCO World Heritage Site and the country's top tourism draw, was among the hardest-hit destinations, with local guides and vendors reporting near-total loss of income during what should have been peak season.
- The Jordanian government and tourism authorities launched reassurance campaigns emphasizing that Jordan remains physically distant from active fighting and that its borders and sites are safe — yet those messages largely failed to overcome travelers' broad perception of the region as dangerous.

> Jordan's tourism industry has been severely impacted by the broader regional instability, even though the country itself was not directly involved in the conflicts.
